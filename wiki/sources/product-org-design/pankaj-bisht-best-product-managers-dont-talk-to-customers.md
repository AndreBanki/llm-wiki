## Summary

The article's core claim is that high-performing PMs do not treat customer interviews as direct roadmap instructions. Customers usually describe pain accurately but prescribe incremental fixes bounded by current mental models. The PM's role is to convert those pain signals into strategic bets using broader context: market movement, adjacent-domain patterns, technology shifts, and product telemetry.

It names a recurring failure mode: teams that keep shipping plausible customer-requested improvements can still become irrelevant when the category itself is being redefined. The essay calls this the incremental trap.

It also criticizes low-quality research practices where interviews are used to legitimize pre-decided roadmap choices. In this framing, rigorous PM practice requires triangulating stated feedback with observed behavior and usage data, then preserving the right to reject requested features.

The article resolves its own provocative title by widening the definition of customer contact: support tickets, complaint channels, sales objections, usability testing, and deep relationships with a small set of high-signal customers. The stance is not anti-customer; it is anti-literal interpretation of requests.

---

## Core Claims

### 1. Pain vs. Prescription Distinction
Customers are generally reliable at expressing friction, but unreliable at prescribing non-incremental solutions.

### 2. Incremental Trap
Roadmaps can be locally rational and globally obsolete when every cycle is optimized for direct request response.

### 3. Research Integrity Risk
Customer interviews often degrade into confirmation exercises unless teams actively test for disconfirming evidence.

### 4. Continuous Multi-Channel Listening
Effective PMs monitor behavioral and operational signal streams, not only scripted interview sessions.

## Caveats and Limits

- The source is opinionated and anecdotal, not an empirical study.
- Historical references (Ford, Jobs) are rhetorical devices, not rigorous causal evidence.
- Should be used as a decision-quality lens, not as a rule to reduce customer contact.
