The distinction between coaching for performance (task-focused) and coaching for development (person-focused), as articulated by Michael Bungay Stanier.

---

## Definition

"Coaching" here is used broadly: any moment you're guiding, supporting, managing, parenting, mentoring, or trying to help someone do their best thinking — not just formal coaching with a certification.

---

## The Two Modes

### Performance Coaching

Focuses on **the task at hand**.

- Primary questions: *How do we get this done? How do we fix the problem? How do we move faster?*
- Default under pressure: most people slip into this mode when stakes are high
- Risk: the coach takes ownership of the thinking, leaving the other person feeling unaccomplished

### Development Coaching

Focuses on **the person doing the task**.

- Primary questions: *Who are they becoming? What are they learning? What capability are they building?*
- Not slow for the sake of being slow; not soft or hands-off
- Requires staying curious long enough for the other person to grow

#### What development coaching looks like in practice

| Behavior | What it means |
|---|---|
| Resisting the urge to rescue | Ask a question, then don't fill the silence with your answer |
| Holding space | Sit with awkward silence instead of jumping in |
| Helping someone think | Facilitate their reasoning, don't supply your conclusion |

---

## The Default Problem

Under high stakes, people default to performance mode: they jump in, offer advice, tighten control, and try to make things efficient. The cost is that the other person never builds capability — and is left feeling unaccomplished.

The shift required is **intentional**: choose development even when performance feels more urgent.

---

## Practical Technique

Before offering a solution, ask:

> **"What have you already considered?"**

Then pause. Capability is often already present — it just needs to be invited forward.

This one question:
- Signals respect for the other person's thinking
- Surfaces existing knowledge before adding your own
- Keeps you from solving a problem the other person could solve themselves
